## Laws of Change

Five fundamental principles explaining why change is not easy:

1. **Organizations don't change, people change** -- change happens when people behave differently because they recognize the value in doing so
2. **People don't resist change; they resist being changed** -- individuals are more likely to change if engaged in defining the change and understanding the vision
3. **Things are the way they are because they got that way** -- understanding origins of current practices helps avoid past mistakes; give staff a voice
4. **Unless there is push to change, things will likely stay the same** -- "You can't solve a problem with the level of thinking that created it" (Einstein)
5. **Change would be easy if it weren't for all the people** -- the technology of change is often easy; the challenge is natural variation in people

Change requires **Change Agents** who actively listen to employees, customers, and stakeholders, plus a clear **VISION** communicated vividly and regularly.

## Bridges's Transition Model

William Bridges emphasizes that change involves managing a **transition** -- the psychological process people go through to come to terms with the new situation. The **single biggest reason organizational changes fail** is that people driving change rarely think about endings.

### Three Transition Phases

| Phase | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **The Ending** | Acknowledging things we need to let go of; recognizing losses (friends, status, familiar ways) | Changing jobs -- even a chosen change involves loss of close working friends |
| **The Neutral Zone** | Old way has finished but new way isn't here yet; everything in flux; confusing and disorderly | Moving to a new house -- first months are turmoil, not yet "home" |
| **The New Beginning** | New way feels comfortable, right, and the only way | Having a baby -- cannot imagine life without the new addition |

### Key Principles
- All individuals go through all three phases, but **at different speeds**
- Progression depends on: past experience, personal style, degree of involvement, feeling of voluntariness
- The Neutral Zone is the **least predictable and most confusing**
- If people don't transition through the Neutral Zone, the organization risks **slipping back into old habits**

### Checklist for Managing Transition

**The Ending:**
- Help everyone understand current problems and why change is necessary
- Identify who is likely to lose what; accept subjective losses
- Expect and accept signs of grieving
- Define what is over and what is not
- Treat the past with respect
- Give information repeatedly in multiple ways

**The Neutral Zone:**
- Recognize this as a difficult but necessary phase
- Get people involved; give time/space to experiment
- Help people feel valued; praise good ideas
- Provide feedback on results

**The New Beginning:**
- Do not force a beginning before its time
- Ensure people know their part in the new system
- Make policies, procedures, and priorities clear
- Celebrate the new beginning; give credit to those who made the change

## Kotter's Eight Errors of Change Management

### Error 1: Allowing Too Much Complacency
- Biggest mistake: plunging ahead without establishing urgency
- Change Agents often: overestimate ability to force change, underestimate difficulty of shifting comfort zones, rush without sufficient communication, confuse urgency with anxiety

### Error 2: Failing to Create a Sufficiently Powerful Guiding Coalition
- Major change is impossible without active support from organizational head
- Without top leader commitment, short-term self-interest outweighs long-term benefits

### Error 3: Underestimating the Power of Vision
- Without a public vision, every choice becomes a debate
- Vision is NOT the project plan -- it is a **clear and compelling statement of where the change is leading**

### Error 4: Under Communicating the Vision by a Factor of 10, 100, or 1000
- Communication in both words and deeds; congruence is critical
- "Do as I say, not as I do" kills change efforts

### Error 5: Permitting Obstacles to Block the Vision
- **Psychological** roadblocks (fear, lack of knowledge)
- **Structural** roadblocks (narrow job categories, misaligned performance appraisals)
- **Active resistance** (people who refuse to adapt and make incompatible demands)

### Error 6: Failing to Create Short-Term Wins
- Complex change requires short-term goals to maintain momentum
- Key: **create** wins rather than merely hope for them

### Error 7: Declaring Victory Too Soon
- Suggestion that the job is done before changes are embedded in culture is a mistake
- Until embedded, new approaches are fragile and old habits can reassert
- Classic "Mission Accomplished" syndrome (e.g., "We bought a DQ tool. That's fixed that now.")

### Error 8: Neglecting to Anchor Changes in Corporate Culture
- Until new behaviors are embedded in social norms and shared values, they decay when focus is removed
- Two keys: show how behaviors influenced performance; embed change in next generation of management
- Improvements in DM are delivered through **processes, people, and technology** -- the people component is often missed

## Kotter's Eight-Step Process for Major Change

| Step | Description | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1. **Establish Urgency** | Create rallying call; overcome complacency | Soften entrenched status quo |
| 2. **Create Guiding Coalition** | Powerful team of volunteers from across org | Soften entrenched status quo |
| 3. **Develop Vision and Strategy** | Clear, compelling direction for change | Soften entrenched status quo |
| 4. **Communicate the Vision** | Simple, repeated, multi-forum, lead by example | Soften entrenched status quo |
| 5. **Empower Broad-Based Action** | Remove obstacles (psychological, structural, resistance) | Introduce new practices |
| 6. **Create Short-Term Wins** | Achieve early goals; reward the team | Introduce new practices |
| 7. **Consolidate Gains** | Produce more change; don't declare victory too soon | Introduce new practices |
| 8. **Anchor in Culture** | Embed new behaviors in social norms and values | Lock changes in place |

- Steps 1-4: Soften the status quo
- Steps 5-7: Introduce new practices
- Step 8: Lock changes in place
- **No shortcut** -- all eight steps must be followed in order
- Use quick wins to bolster vision and communication at each step

## Sources of Complacency (Kotter)

Nine reasons organizations/people are complacent:
1. Absence of a visible crisis
2. Trappings of success drowning out urgency
3. Low overall performance standards
4. Overly narrow functional goals
5. Internal systems rigged/gamed to make goals easy
6. Only internal performance feedback (no external sanity check)
7. "Kill the messenger" culture -- problems attacked rather than addressed
8. Psychological denial -- people don't accept what they don't want to hear
9. "Happy talk" from senior management creating false sense of security

## The Guiding Coalition

### Two Scenarios to Avoid
- **Lone CEO / Lone Champion**: Success/failure in one person's hands; decision-making slows
- **Low Credibility Committee**: Lacks senior-level representation; seen as "not that important"

### Four Key Characteristics
1. **Position Power**: Enough key players so opponents can't easily block progress
2. **Expertise**: Relevant viewpoints adequately represented
3. **Credibility**: Enough people with good reputations to be taken seriously
4. **Leadership**: Enough proven leaders to drive the change process

### Behaviors to Avoid
- **Naysaying**: Hampers positive dialogue and creative ideas
- **Distraction**: Takes team off course; delays or missed opportunities
- **Selfishness**: Hidden agendas derailing the team's efforts

### Personality Types to Watch
- **Egos**: Fill the room, don't let others contribute
- **Snakes**: Create and spread mistrust
- **Reluctant Players**: See moderate need but don't grasp urgency

### Combating Group Think
- Encourage scientific method of gathering data
- Develop criteria for evaluating all decisions
- Leaders should speak last
- Actively search for outside knowledge
- Develop Plan B to force rethinking of assumptions

## Developing and Communicating Vision

### Why Vision is Essential
- **Clarification**: Simplifies decisions by setting key parameters; "Is this in line with the vision?"
- **Motivation**: Motivates people to take steps even when personally painful
- **Alignment**: Coordinates actions of motivated people efficiently

### Characteristics of an Effective Vision
- **Imaginable**: Conveys a picture of the future
- **Desirable**: Appeals to long-term interests of all stakeholders
- **Feasible**: Realistic and attainable goals
- **Focused**: Clear enough for decision-making guidance
- **Flexible**: General enough for initiative and alternative plans
- **Communicable**: Can be shared in five minutes or less

### Seven Elements of Effective Communication
1. **Keep it simple** -- strip out jargon
2. **Use metaphor, analogy, and example** -- verbal picture worth 1000 words
3. **Use multiple forums** -- elevator pitch to all-hands briefing
4. **Repeat, repeat, repeat** -- ideas must be heard many times
5. **Lead by example** -- behavior consistent with vision; inconsistency overwhelms all other communication
6. **Explain seeming inconsistencies** -- simple, clear, honest rationale
7. **Give and take** -- two-way communication more powerful than one-way

### Walking the Talk
- Leadership by example makes values tangible
- Telling one thing and doing the opposite sends a clear message that the vision can be ignored
- Stephen Covey: "Seek first to understand, then to be understood"

## The Gleicher Formula for Change

**C = D x V x F > R**

- **C** = Change
- **D** = Dissatisfaction with status quo
- **V** = Vision of a better alternative
- **F** = First actionable steps
- **R** = Resistance to change

The product of D, V, and F must overcome R. If any one of D, V, or F is zero, the product is zero and change will not happen.

### Cautions
- Increasing dissatisfaction must be wielded carefully to avoid increasing resistance
- Vision must be concrete -- what will people do differently, stop doing, or start doing
- First steps must be achievable and tied back to the vision
- Avoid alienating people -- requires good understanding of stakeholders

## Diffusion of Innovations (Rogers)

### Five Adopter Categories

| Category | % of Population | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Innovators** | 2.5% | First to spot better ways; high risk tolerance; use own resources |
| **Early Adopters** | 13.5% | Highest opinion leadership; "visionary" managers; formalize practices |
| **Early Majority** | 34% | Slower adoption; above average social status; seldom opinion leaders |
| **Late Majority** | 34% | High skepticism; below average social status; tight budgets |
| **Laggards** | 16% | Last to adopt; averse to change agents; focus on traditions |

### Two Critical Challenge Points
1. **Breaking past Early Adopters** to reach the "Tipping Point" where innovation becomes mainstream
2. **Moving from Late Majority to Laggards** -- accept that 100% conversion may not be possible

### Four Key Elements of Diffusion
1. **Innovation**: An idea, practice, or object perceived as new
2. **Communication Channels**: Means by which messages travel
3. **Time**: Speed of adoption
4. **Social System**: Interrelated units engaged in joint problem solving

### Five Stages of Adoption
1. **Knowledge**: First exposure; lacks information; not yet inspired to learn more
2. **Persuasion**: Actively seeks information about the innovation
3. **Decision**: Weighs advantages/disadvantages; chooses to adopt or reject
4. **Implementation**: Employs the innovation; determines usefulness
5. **Confirmation**: Finalizes decision to continue; may use to fullest potential

### Factors Affecting Acceptance
- **Relative Advantage**: Does it offer advantage over previous way?
- **Compatibility**: Fits life, way of working, culture
- **Complexity/Simplicity**: Easier to use = more likely adopted
- **Trialability**: Ease of experimenting (e.g., freemium offers)
- **Observability**: Visibility drives communication through networks

## Sustaining Change

- Common problem: initiatives launched in response to a symptom; as symptom is addressed, urgency lessens
- Maintain sense of urgency; be alert to emerging areas of dissatisfaction
- **Don't confuse project scope with change vision** -- many projects may be needed to achieve vision
- Broaden the Guiding Coalition beyond immediately affected stakeholders
- Demonstrate how principles and tools are **transferrable** to other initiatives

### Vision Framing Example
- **Narrow** (objective): "Implement a governance framework for personal data to comply with EU rules"
- **Broad** (vision): "Lead our industry in repeatable and scalable approaches for managing critical information assets to ensure profits, reduce risks, improve quality of service"

## Communicating Data Management Value

### Communication Principles
- Clear objective and desired outcome
- Key messages supporting the desired outcome
- Tailored to audience/stakeholders
- Appropriate media for the audience

### Communication Goals
- Informing, Educating, Setting goals/vision, Defining solutions, Promoting change, Influencing action, Gaining feedback, Generating support

### Audience Engagement Tactics
- **Solve problems** pertinent to stakeholder needs
- **Address pain points** specific to each group (compliance = risk; marketing = opportunities)
- **Present changes as improvements** from which stakeholders benefit
- **Have a vision of success** describing future state impact
- **Avoid jargon** -- technical emphasis turns people off
- **Share stories and examples** -- analogies help memory
- **Recognize fear as motivation** -- consequences of not managing data

### Communication Plan Elements
- Message, Goal/Objective, Audience, Style, Channel/Method/Medium, Timing, Frequency, Materials, Communicators, Expected Response, Metrics, Budget/Resource Plan

### The Human Element
- People are influenced by colleagues and leaders
- Use stakeholder analysis to find groups with like interests
- As support broadens, supporters share the message with peers

### Keep Communicating
- DM is ongoing, not one-time
- New employees, changing roles -- refresh communications
- Competition for funding never goes away
- Show progress and celebrate successes
- Over time, knowledge of data's importance changes organizational thinking

## Exam/Test Patterns (Frequently Tested)

- **Five Laws of Change** -- especially "organizations don't change, people change" and "people resist being changed, not change itself"
- **Bridges's three transition phases**: Ending, Neutral Zone, New Beginning -- and the single biggest failure reason (not managing endings)
- **Kotter's Eight Errors** -- especially complacency (#1), under-communicating vision (#4), declaring victory too soon (#7), not anchoring in culture (#8)
- **Kotter's Eight Steps** -- order matters; steps 1-4 soften status quo; 5-7 introduce new practices; 8 locks in change
- **Nine sources of complacency** -- especially "kill the messenger" and "happy talk"
- **Guiding Coalition** characteristics: Position Power, Expertise, Credibility, Leadership
- **Lone CEO** and **Low Credibility Committee** -- two scenarios to avoid
- **Effective vision characteristics**: Imaginable, Desirable, Feasible, Focused, Flexible, Communicable
- **Gleicher Formula**: C = D x V x F > R (if any factor is zero, no change)
- **Rogers's Diffusion of Innovations**: five adopter categories and percentages; Tipping Point concept
- **Five stages of adoption**: Knowledge, Persuasion, Decision, Implementation, Confirmation
- **Factors affecting acceptance**: Relative Advantage, Compatibility, Complexity, Trialability, Observability
- **"Mission Accomplished" syndrome** -- declaring victory when technology is deployed but people haven't changed
- **Walking the Talk** -- nothing undermines vision more than leaders acting incongruently
- **Communication plan elements** -- Message, Audience, Channel, Timing, Frequency, Metrics
